When You Want to Edit $PATH variables . . .

Published: Thu 18 December 2014
By Fulton Byrne

In When You Want . . ..

First off, know that whenever you type $PATH in the command line, even if it's for the title of some markdown you're writing in vim, bash will interperet it as the variable unless you escape the $ like so \$.

Typically, our friends on the internet will tell you to do the following command when you want to add something to your path . . .

export desired/path/to/binary/file:$PATH

This just adds that path to your .bashrc, which in Ubuntu is in your user home directory. Which for me is /fulton/home. It's a hidden file so you'll have to do ls -a to ferry it out.

Here's what the top of my .bashrc looks like . . .

export PATH="/fulton/Programs/Komodo-IDE-6.1.2-x86/bin:$PATH"
export PATH="/usr/local/src/phantomjs-1.6.0-linux-i686-dynamic/bin:$PATH"
export PATH="$PATH:$HOME/node_modules/less/bin"

Yuck, Komodo?! Is that even cool anymore? Backspace, backspace, ..., backspace, and now Komodo is no longer added to my path whenever I login as me. I should also delete that nasty booger as well.
